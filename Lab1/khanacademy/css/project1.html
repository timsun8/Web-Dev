Galapagos Islands

The Galápagos Islands are an archipelago of volcanic islands located in the Pacific Ocean, approximately 1,000 kilometers from the coast of Ecuador. These islands are famous for their unique biodiversity and played a significant role in Charles Darwin's development of the theory of evolution by natural selection. The islands are home to species that can't be found anywhere else in the world, such as the Galápagos tortoise and the marine iguana. The Galápagos National Park and Marine Reserve are UNESCO World Heritage sites, providing protection to the delicate ecosystems found there.

What you can see...
  • Endemic species like the Galápagos tortoise and marine iguana.
  • Unique volcanic landscapes and crystal-clear waters.
  • Rare birds, such as the blue-footed booby and frigatebird.

Antelope Canyon

Antelope Canyon is a unique natural formation located in the state of Arizona, USA. It is a narrow canyon known for its stunning shapes and light effects that occur when sunlight hits its sandstone walls. The canyon is divided into two main sections — Upper and Lower, both attracting tourists with their beauty and mystical atmosphere. Visiting the canyon requires guided tours, as the terrain can be quite dangerous and requires special knowledge for safe navigation.

What you can see...
  • Flowing, wave-like sandstone formations.
  • Light beams that shine through narrow passages, creating beautiful color effects.
  • Intricate patterns and shadows along the canyon walls.

The Spanish Civil War (1936-1939) was a pivotal conflict that led to the rise of Francisco Franco's fascist regime in Spain. The war was fought between the Republican forces, which included communists, anarchists, and other left-wing groups, and the Nationalist forces, led by Franco. The war caused significant loss of life and had a lasting impact on Spanish society and politics. After Franco's victory, Spain remained under authoritarian rule until his death in 1975, which eventually led to the transition to democracy.
